**Mechanical interpretation of the shift.** Many trades now labeled
`stop_loss` are BE saves: the post-fix BE arm moves the stop to entry
± 1 tick after PT1 touch, so the eventual "stop hit" terminal event
fires at a small positive P&L. Sample inspection confirms (one
`stop_loss` trade with net=+$0.50, MFE=$35, MAE=−$4.50). This
mechanically explains the joint rise in win rate and stop_loss exit
count.

## Distribution shape change

R-unit redefined. The pre-fix R = average net P&L of `stop_loss`-tagged
trades ≈ $15.93. Post-fix that bucket includes BE saves, dragging the
average down to ≈ $11.56. The aggregate `average_loss_cents` (across
all losing trades) is essentially unchanged: $15.89 → $15.87. The
R-unit redefinition is the cleanest invariant for Kelly analysis, but
the dollar-denominated comparisons below use the same dollar baseline
across pre/post to keep apples-to-apples.

| Distribution moment | Pre-fix | Post-fix | Δ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| E[R per trade] | 0.237 R | 0.284 R | +0.047 (+19.8%) |
| Std[R per trade] | 1.603 R | 2.010 R | +0.407 (+25.4%) |
| Skew (informal) | right-tailed (b=2.11) | more symmetric (b=1.38) | flatter |
| Avg win | 2.107 R ($33.57) | 1.901 R ($21.97) | −0.206 R |
| Avg loss (in R-units) | 0.997 R ($15.89) | 1.374 R ($15.87) | larger in R-units (R shrank) |
| Win/loss ratio b | 2.113 | 1.384 | −0.729 |

The key shape change: **post-fix distribution has lower b but higher
win rate, with materially higher variance (+25.4%).** The BE saves
shifted left-tail losses into small positives, which raises win rate
and E[R] but also adds variance (the new "win" cluster sits near zero,
spreading the dispersion).

## Kelly headline: classic and generalized diverge

| Method | Pre-fix | Post-fix | Δ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Classic binary `(pb−q)/b` | 11.26% | **14.92%** | **+3.66pp** |
| Generalized log-utility (full distribution) | 10.29% | **7.89%** | **−2.40pp** |
| Operator back-of-envelope (provided in dispatch) | — | ~16.5% (b≈1.45) | aligns with classic |

**The two Kelly methods disagreed pre-fix by 0.97pp; they disagree
post-fix by 7.03pp.** The divergence is the most important finding in
this re-derivation.

Mechanically: classic Kelly is a binary-outcome approximation that
collapses every win to `avg_win` and every loss to `avg_loss`. It
weights only `p` and `b`. When `p` rises (39.77% → 50.61%) and `b`
falls (2.11 → 1.38), the formula's response is dominated by the `p·b`
term and the result rises.

Generalized Kelly maximizes `E[log(1 + f·R)]` over the actual return
distribution. It accounts for variance directly. The post-fix variance
rose 25.4% — enough to overwhelm the E[R] gain of 19.8% in the
log-utility framework. Result: optimal f* falls.

**The generalized Kelly is the correct answer for a real fixed-fraction
sizing decision.** The classic formula is informative as an upper-bound
proxy but materially overstates sizing capacity when variance is
non-trivially asymmetric.

## Bootstrap CIs (N=5000, post-fix)

| Metric | Point | 5% | Median | 95% |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| PF (i.i.d.) | 1.418 | 1.194 | 1.415 | 1.664 |
| PF (block-20, preserves serial structure) | 1.418 | 1.170 | 1.400 | 1.662 |
| Classic Kelly | 14.92% | 7.77% | 14.86% | 21.43% |
| Generalized Kelly | 7.89% | 3.93% | 7.84% | 11.70% |

- P(PF < 1.0) i.i.d.: **0.02%** (vs pre-fix 19.72% on Pine analyses;
  this strategy was already cleanly positive pre-fix at 0% path-aware)
- P(PF < 1.0) block-20: **0.16%**
- P(net < 0) i.i.d.: **0.02%**
- P(net < 0) block-20: **0.16%**
- P(classic Kelly < 0): **0.02%**
- P(classic Kelly < 5%): **1.16%**
- **P(generalized Kelly < 5%): 11.26%** ← the meaningful tail

The post-fix distribution is more reliably positive than pre-fix
across nearly every robustness metric. **However, the generalized
Kelly's tail risk is wider than pre-fix** (P(generalized < 5%) went
from 4.25% → 11.26%). This is the variance signal: even though edge
robustness is up, the *honest sizing ceiling* tightened.

**Honest sizing ceiling: 5th percentile of bootstrapped generalized
Kelly = 3.93%.** About half of the point estimate (~half-Kelly), which
is the standard practitioner discount for parameter uncertainty.
Quarter-Kelly = 1.97% remains the conservative-ceiling reference.

## Tiered sizing simulations on $50k account

Same trade sequence as the held-out walk-forward (chronological order
preserved).

| Sizing scheme | Final $ | CAGR (3mo) | Max DD% |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Flat 1% | $224,868 | 349.7% | 30.80% |
| Flat 2% | $807,319 | 1514.6% | 54.98% |

| Per-tier half-Kelly | $52,197,006 | 104,294.0% | 61.62% |
| 1% × (tier_f / mean_tier_f) | $487,442 | 874.9% | **19.95%** |
| 2% × (tier_f / mean_tier_f) | $3,262,905 | 6,425.8% | 36.58% |

The **1% baseline × tier-edge tilt** scheme remains the standout for
risk-adjusted performance: 2.2× the flat-1% return at materially lower
max DD (19.95% vs 30.80%). This pattern is consistent with the pre-fix
result, just at slightly different absolute levels.

Per-tier quarter-Kelly (capping aggressive tier-A while floor-capping
the weaker tiers) produced ~$3.2M on the 3-month sample at 36% max DD
— directionally promising but the in-sample CAGR is not a forward
expectation; it's exposure to one walk-forward path.

## What this evidence supports and does not support

**Supports (for a future ADR-0016 sizing-amendment evidence pack):**

1. **The pre-fix Kelly conclusions are not preserved post-fix.** The
   pre-fix coordinator-session research recommended "0.5% per trade
   (≈16 contracts on $50k)" as a step-up; the post-fix substrate
   shifts that recommendation — the honest sizing ceiling (5%-tile
   generalized) tightened from 5.23% → 3.93%.
2. **Generalized Kelly should be the canonical sizing reference, not
   classic Kelly.** The 7.03pp divergence post-fix is too large to
   ignore. Any amendment proposal that cites only the classic formula
   is materially over-sizing.
3. **Tier-tilted sizing has structural support post-fix.** A_open
   remains the dominant edge tier; D_afternoon improved but is still
   below A_open. The tilt-around-flat-1% scheme delivers the best
   in-sample risk-adjusted result and would be the simplest
   amendment-grade specification.
4. **Sample size for amendment-grade evidence is still 3 months.** The
   bootstrap P(generalized Kelly < 5%) of 11.26% means in 1-in-9
   alternate-history paths, the true sizing capacity is below
   half-Kelly. CF-44 anti-near-miss-tuning applies analogously to
   sizing: in-sample favorable resamples should not drive
   recommendation.

**Does NOT support (this memo is research-tier only):**

1. **A specific sizing parameter for ADR-0016.** The memo provides the
   evidence framework; the amendment proposal proper requires a
   separate dispatch with external-methodological justification per
   CF-45 (threshold revisions require external methodological
   justification — by analogy, sizing revisions require the same).
2. **A re-derivation of ADR-0016 thresholds.** This memo is bounded to
   sizing analysis; threshold revisions are out of scope.
3. **A new validation gate or strategy-id change.** Per ADR-0024
   LD-024-4, re-derivation does not regenerate strategy IDs or modify
   parameter locks; this memo inherits that bound.

## Deferred questions (carry-forward to amendment-cycle work)

1. **Forward-out-of-sample validation.** The bootstrap is in-sample.
   A real sizing decision needs OOS data — minimum 6 months of paper
   trading (per CF-52 floor) before any amendment-grade sizing
   recommendation can be cited.
2. **Slippage at higher contract counts.** At quarter-Kelly on $50k
   (~22 contracts) MNQ liquidity absorbs cleanly. At half-Kelly (~43
   contracts) the bootstrap-corrected sizing is still tractable. The
   in-sample sims that produce $3M+ CAGR rely on contract counts
   (200+) that would materially impact fills. Slippage modeling is a
   separate research task.
3. **Path-aware drawdown vs Kelly-implied DD.** The Kelly framework
   maximizes geometric mean log return but is silent on path-dependent
   ruin probability beyond the bootstrap. A separate analysis using
   the Halford / Vince partial-Kelly survival framework would tighten
   the "honest sizing ceiling" further.
4. **Tier-stability across regimes.** The tier-edge structure
   (A_open dominant) is consistent pre-fix and post-fix on the same
   Feb-Apr 2026 corpus. Whether the structure persists across
   different volatility regimes is an open question — the
   `vix_percentile_bucket` analysis from the earlier coordinator
   research is the relevant cross-reference.
5. **Multi-strategy sizing interaction.** If a future cycle dispatches
   a second ADVANCE-grade strategy (e.g., a Cycle4 MOC family
   candidate), the sizing question becomes a portfolio one. Single-
   strategy Kelly does not extend cleanly to multi-strategy contexts;
   correlation structure must be modeled.

## Caveats and bounds

- **In-sample only.** All numbers in this memo are derived from the
  Feb-Mar-Apr 2026 held-out corpus. No OOS evidence.
- **R-unit redefinition.** Post-fix R = $11.56 (vs pre-fix $15.93) is
  a consequence of BE saves entering the `stop_loss` exit bucket.
  Dollar-denominated comparisons remain valid; R-denominated
  comparisons require care.
- **Methodology preserved.** Per ADR-0024 LD-024-4, no parameter lock,
  strategy code, or regime substrate was modified. The substrate is
  the canonical re-derivation output.
- **Single-strategy scope.** This memo analyzes
  `regime_shock_reversion_short_v2` only. The two REJECTed Cycle3
  strategies (`vwap_overnight_reversal_long/short`) are not in scope.
- **No live-promotion blockage.** P3 priority confirms; CF-52 paper-
  observation window remains the canonical bottleneck for live
  promotion.
